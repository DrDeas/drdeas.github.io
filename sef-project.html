The Problem
Why this, why now

Across the South, school systems are making consequential AI adoption decisions with little systematic examination of how those choices affect historically underserved students. In the five public school systems at the center of this study, AI strategies range from the nation's first AI-dedicated high school to a complete block on AI tools — and that variation maps almost directly onto race and poverty.

RAND's 2024–25 research documents the national pattern: high-poverty districts are half as likely to receive AI professional development as low-poverty ones. The Brookings Institution (2025) warns of "banal deception" — where AI's conversational fluency causes students to mistake algorithmic outputs for human judgment, with particular consequences for students who already receive less adult guidance and support.

General-purpose AI auditing tools require programming expertise few educators possess. No education-specific, non-technical, Southern-equity-centered auditing tool exists anywhere in the field.

The students facing the greatest algorithmic risk have the fewest resources to name, document, or contest it. This study changes that.

— Study rationale, Faces at the Bottom of the Context Window
The Project
Community as co-researcher — not subject

This study uses Community-Based Participatory Action Research (CBPAR) to examine how AI systems affect historically marginalized students across five Southern school districts. Students, families, and educators are positioned as co-researchers — generating AI Accountability Stories that capture what aggregate data cannot see.

The primary deliverable is a validated, open-access AI Equity Auditing Framework — a practical toolkit that non-technical educators and community advocates can use independently to evaluate AI systems in their own schools.

The framework evaluates five domains — hover to explore:

  • 01 Community voice in AI procurement decisions
  • 02 Transparency and explainability of AI outputs
  • 03 Data privacy and equity protections
  • 04 Monitoring for disparate impact across student groups
  • 05 Ongoing accountability and community redress mechanisms

Published under Creative Commons and disseminated through CCSSO, SEF, and the EDSAFE AI Alliance — reaching district leaders, teachers, and advocates nationwide.

Theory of Change Click or hover each step to expand
Step 01 AI Accountability Stories 30–40 interviews and 8–10 focus groups across five districts document how AI shapes daily educational life for marginalized students and families — in their own words.
Step 02 Institutional Landscape Analysis Cross-district policy review and educator questionnaire (40–50 respondents via Qualtrics) surface how institutional conditions shape AI adoption and who bears the costs.
Step 03 Framework Co-Development Community review panel (6–8 members) and practitioner panel (4–5 district staff) validate a five-domain auditing framework. Community ownership is built in from the start.
Step 04 Open-Access Publication Creative Commons framework + 10–12 page plain-language practitioner guide, published through CCSSO AI Resource Hub, SEF networks, and EDSAFE AI Alliance.
Outcome Community-Led AI Accountability Non-technical educators and families in any district can independently audit AI tools affecting their students — without external expertise, budget, or institutional permission.
