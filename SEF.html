The Critical Gap

AI is transforming education—but without equity safeguards.

Southern states are rapidly adopting AI-powered systems for student assessment, behavioral monitoring, college readiness prediction, and personalized learning. But who's checking for bias?

Rapid, Unchecked Adoption

The Southern Regional Education Board's AI Commission reports accelerating AI adoption across 16 member states—but no systematic equity auditing frameworks exist for K-12 educational tools.

Missing Community Voice

Current AI evaluation approaches require technical programming skills, excluding the very communities most affected: students, families, and educators in under-resourced schools.

Documented Disparate Impact

Research confirms AI tools exhibit bias against Black, Latino, Indigenous, and neurodivergent students. 25% of Southern children lack broadband access, compounding algorithmic discrimination with digital divide.

Guiding Questions

What We're Investigating

This research is driven by questions that center community experience and institutional capacity.

Primary Research Question

How can Southern educational communities develop accessible, open-source methodologies to identify, document, and address algorithmic bias in AI-powered educational systems?

1

What are the patterns of algorithmic harm experienced by Black, Latino, Indigenous, and other marginalized students across different AI educational tools in Southern schools?

2

How do community members (students, families, educators) conceptualize and describe AI-driven decision-making systems affecting educational outcomes?

3

What institutional barriers prevent effective equity evaluation of AI tools during procurement and implementation processes in Southern state education agencies and districts?

4

What forms of documentation and evidence are most effective for influencing policy change and vendor accountability?

5

How can student-led auditing initiatives create sustainable mechanisms for ongoing AI accountability?

Research Design

Community-Based Participatory Action Research

Three integrated methodological components designed for accessibility, rigor, and real-world impact.

1

AI Accountability Stories

Qualitative Documentation
  • 50-75 in-depth interviews across 5 Southern states
  • 15-20 focus groups with students, families, educators
  • Digital storytelling & photovoice workshops
  • Systematic qualitative coding (MAXQDA, ATLAS.ti)
  • Community Advisory Board oversight
2

Institutional Capacity Assessment

Mixed-Methods Analysis
  • Document analysis of AI procurement policies (10 states)
  • Key informant interviews (25-30) with state officials
  • Educator survey (n=200-300) on AI awareness
  • Case studies of 3-5 districts
  • Identification of practical implementation barriers
3

Open-Source Framework Development

Tool Creation & Piloting
  • AI Equity Audit Toolkit (interview protocols, guides)
  • Community Auditing Playbook (step-by-step)
  • Student AI Auditor Curriculum (youth-led research)
  • Digital repository (Creative Commons license)
  • Pilot testing in 8-10 schools/districts

This research directly supports the Southern Education Foundation's core commitment to education justice through multiple strategic pathways.

Centering Marginalized Communities

SEF's 160-year legacy focuses on advancing equity for historically underserved students in the South. This research centers the voices of Black, Latino, Indigenous, and neurodivergent students whose educational opportunities are increasingly shaped by AI systems—without their input or consent.

  • Community-based participatory action research
  • Student-led auditing initiatives
  • Youth empowerment through research training

Authentic Southern Focus

Most AI accountability work centers coastal, urban districts. This research addresses the unique challenges of Southern education systems: rural digital divides, historical segregation patterns, concentrated poverty, and state education agencies with limited technical capacity.

  • 5-state research scope (AL, GA, MS, NC, SC)
  • Partnership with Southern Regional Education Board
  • Former SEF fellow (2012) returning with expertise

Practical, Actionable Tools

SEF emphasizes research that drives policy change and institutional improvement. This project creates open-source tools that state education agencies, districts, and advocates can immediately use to evaluate AI systems—filling a critical gap identified by CCSSO members.

  • Accessible methodology (no programming required)
  • Designed for under-resourced settings
  • Built-in dissemination through CCSSO, SREB networks

Timely, Emerging Issue

SEF seeks research addressing urgent equity challenges in Southern education. AI adoption is accelerating—SREB's AI Commission released recommendations in 2024, states are developing policies now—but equity safeguards lag behind. This research intervenes at a critical moment.

  • Aligns with SREB AI Commission priorities
  • Addresses gap in CCSSO AI Resource Hub
  • Creates evidence base for policy development

Research Foundations

Standing on the Shoulders of Critical Scholarship

This work builds on established research documenting algorithmic bias, educational inequity, and the need for community-centered technology evaluation.

Algorithmic Bias in Education

Holmes et al. (2022) called for community-wide ethics frameworks for AI in education, emphasizing the need for participatory approaches that center affected communities. Koenecke et al. (2020) demonstrated racial disparities in automated speech recognition, highlighting how AI systems perpetuate existing inequalities.

Read Holmes et al. (2022)

Race After Technology & Automating Inequality

Benjamin (2019) and Eubanks (2018) provide critical frameworks for understanding how technological systems encode and amplify existing social inequalities, particularly affecting marginalized communities.

Explore Benjamin's Framework

Southern Digital Divide

SREB (2024) reports 25% of school-aged children in the South lack broadband access, disproportionately affecting Black, Latino, and rural students—compounding algorithmic discrimination with infrastructure inequality.

SREB AI Commission Report

CCSSO + ILO Group AI Framework

ILO Group (2024) Framework for Implementing AI in State Education Agencies identifies equity, transparency, and community engagement as priorities—but provides limited guidance on operationalization. This research fills that gap.

View ILO Framework

Personalized Learning Critique

Watters (2021) and Tseng et al. (2023) critically examine personalized learning claims, documenting how AI-powered systems often reinforce inequities while promising individualization.

Teaching Machines (MIT Press)

Mozilla Trustworthy AI Theory of Change

Mozilla Foundation (2019) articulates vision for AI that serves communities through transparency, accountability, and open-source approaches—directly aligning with this research's methodology and values.

Mozilla AI Investments
