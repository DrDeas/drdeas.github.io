Project Overview

The Challenge We're Addressing

Artificial intelligence is entering Southern classrooms faster than the research or policy frameworks needed to govern it equitably. In metro Atlanta alone, five public school systems serving hundreds of thousands of students have adopted radically different AI strategies ‚Äî ranging from a district-wide AI high school to a complete block on AI tools ‚Äî with little systematic examination of how these choices affect historically underserved students and their communities.

This study examines that uneven landscape through the lens of the communities most affected. Using Community-Based Participatory Action Research, we position students, families, and educators as co-researchers rather than subjects ‚Äî capturing the AI Accountability Stories that reveal how algorithmic systems affect daily educational life in ways that aggregate data alone cannot see.

The primary deliverable is a validated, open-access AI Equity Auditing Framework ‚Äî a practical toolkit that non-technical educators and community advocates can use independently to evaluate AI systems in their own schools, without programming expertise or institutional research infrastructure.

Metro Atlanta offers a rare natural experiment: five demographically diverse districts at radically different stages of AI adoption within a single geographic and policy context. The variation maps almost directly onto race and poverty ‚Äî mirroring national trends documented by RAND showing that high-poverty districts are half as likely to provide AI teacher training as low-poverty ones.

Research Questions

What This Study Asks

RQ 01

How are AI-powered educational tools affecting the daily educational experiences of historically underserved students across metro Atlanta's five public school systems?

RQ 02

How do students, families, and educators understand and make meaning of AI-driven decision-making processes that affect them?

RQ 03

What institutional conditions ‚Äî policies, leadership, resources, and community relationships ‚Äî shape how AI tools are adopted, governed, and evaluated across the five districts?

RQ 04

What framework components are most effective for enabling non-technical community members to conduct meaningful AI equity audits of their local school systems?

Theoretical Framework

How We Think About This Problem

This study sits at the intersection of three robust theoretical traditions, each chosen for its explanatory power and its grounding in community experience rather than abstract analysis.

CBPAR ensures that affected communities drive the research rather than serve as its subjects. Critical Race Theory provides the analytical lens for understanding how AI systems reproduce and amplify existing inequities. Algorithmic Accountability Theory offers the technical framework for evaluating AI governance.

Together, they make it possible to translate complex algorithmic questions into actionable community tools ‚Äî the core purpose of this work.

Anchor Methodology Community-Based Participatory Action Research (CBPAR) ‚Äî Community members as co-researchers, not subjects. Findings emerge from lived experience and are validated by the communities they describe. Rooted in traditions of participatory action research and Southern community organizing.
Analytical Lens Critical Race Theory in Education (Ladson-Billings & Tate, 1995; Solorzano & Yosso, 2002) ‚Äî Examines how race and power shape educational structures, policies, and technologies. Provides tools for identifying when AI "neutrality" encodes historical inequality.
Technical Framework Algorithmic Accountability Theory (Raji et al., 2020; Benjamin, 2019; Noble, 2018) ‚Äî Structures the analysis of how AI systems are designed, audited, and governed. Informs the five-domain evaluation framework that communities will be equipped to use.
Emerging Context Informed by Brookings Institution (2025) on "banal deception" ‚Äî the risk that AI's conversational tone causes students to confuse algorithmic outputs with human judgment. And by RAND (2024‚Äì25) on the persistent equity gap in AI professional development access across income levels.

Research Methods

Three Integrated Research Components

Component 01
AI Accountability Stories: Community Narrative Documentation

The primary data collection method. Qualitative narrative documentation capturing the lived experience of AI in schools from the perspectives of those most affected ‚Äî not administrators or vendors.

  • 30‚Äì40 semi-structured interviews (45‚Äì60 min.), 6‚Äì8 per district, stratified across students, parents, teachers, and tech coordinators
  • 8‚Äì10 focus groups (75‚Äì90 min.), 1‚Äì2 per site, organized by stakeholder type
  • Document collection: AI policies, vendor contracts, professional development materials, equity review records
  • Analysis: MAXQDA iterative thematic coding + member checking with 8 participants
Component 02
Institutional Landscape Analysis

Systematic analysis of how institutional conditions shape ‚Äî and are shaped by ‚Äî AI adoption decisions, with GaDOE's January 2025 guidance as a cross-site reference document.

  • 10‚Äì15 key informant interviews with district leadership across all five sites
  • Structured educator questionnaire (25‚Äì30 items) to 40‚Äì50 classroom teachers via Qualtrics, adapting validated RAND American Teacher Panel items for comparability
  • Document analysis examining the tension between state guidance (non-binding) and classroom practice
Component 03
Framework Co-Development and Validation

The primary deliverable. The AI Equity Auditing Framework is co-developed with community members ‚Äî not handed down from researchers ‚Äî making it far more likely to be used and trusted.

  • Five evaluation domains: student/community voice in procurement, transparency/explainability, data privacy and equity, disparate impact monitoring, and ongoing accountability mechanisms
  • Community review panel (6‚Äì8 participants) and practitioner review panel (4‚Äì5 district staff) in Months 8‚Äì9
  • Published open-access under Creative Commons with 10‚Äì12 page practitioner guide in accessible, non-technical language
  • Disseminated through CCSSO AI Resource Hub, SEF networks, and EDSAFE AI Alliance

The Inside-Outside Perspective

Eldrin Deas brings a combination of credentials that is genuinely unusual in AI equity research: he has worked inside major algorithmic platforms ‚Äî Twitter and LinkedIn ‚Äî as someone responsible for how content curation and engagement systems actually behave at scale, and he has worked inside Southern public schools and state education agencies as someone who understands what those systems do to students and communities. That dual vantage point is the methodological foundation of this project.

At Twitter, as Curation Standards Analysis Lead, he analyzed how the platform's algorithmic systems made decisions about what content to surface, suppress, and flag ‚Äî experience that directly informs his understanding of how AI systems in educational settings may appear neutral while encoding historical patterns of exclusion. At LinkedIn, he examined how engagement algorithms interact with professional networks in ways that can bridge or reinforce social capital gaps.

Deep Southern Educational Roots

Before entering the world of platform accountability, Eldrin spent three years as a Title I mathematics teacher at Towers High School in DeKalb County ‚Äî one of the five districts in this study. He went on to work with the Georgia Department of Education, the Pennsylvania Department of Education, the Texas Education Agency, and Kansas City Public Schools as a consultant on school and district improvement.

His 2012 Southern Education Foundation Fellowship, his 2020 workshop at the CCSSO SCASS Winter Convening co-presented with the GaDOE team, and his ongoing FUSE Corps board leadership all reflect an intentional commitment to working within ‚Äî rather than around ‚Äî the institutional structures of Southern education. This is not research conducted from a distance.

Methodological Approach and Dissemination Strategy

The research draws on qualitative analysis software including MAXQDA and NVivo, combined with Qualtrics and SPSS for the educator questionnaire component. The community narrative methodology ‚Äî "AI Accountability Stories" ‚Äî is grounded in the tradition of participatory action research pioneered by Ladson-Billings, Solorzano and Yosso, and the broader CBPAR literature, adapted here for the specific challenge of making algorithmic accountability accessible to non-technical community advocates.

The dissemination strategy ‚Äî through CCSSO's AI Resource Hub, SEF's networks, and the EDSAFE AI Alliance ‚Äî is designed to ensure that the framework reaches practitioners who can use it, not just researchers who can cite it. A Creative Commons license and plain-language practitioner guide are built into the deliverable design from the outset.
