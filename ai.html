The Problem

AI-powered tools are entering Southern classrooms at accelerating speed — influencing student assessments, instructional recommendations, and disciplinary decisions. No accessible, open-source framework exists to help educators, advocates, or researchers evaluate whether these tools treat all students equitably.

General-purpose auditing tools like IBM's AI Fairness 360 and Microsoft's Fairlearn were built for hiring and criminal justice contexts. Education-focused initiatives remain either vendor-facing or too technical for non-programmers. The result: communities most affected have no practical means to hold these systems accountable.

What This Framework Does
Community-Centered Auditing

A structured methodology using "AI Accountability Stories" — qualitative documentation of how AI systems affect students, families, and educators — translated into actionable equity indicators.

No-Code Accessibility

Designed explicitly for non-programmers. Educators, parent advocates, and community researchers can use the framework without data science backgrounds or technical infrastructure.

Southern Specificity

Grounded in the distinct policy landscape, demographic context, and historical inequities of Southern educational systems — not adapted from frameworks built elsewhere.

Open Infrastructure

Fully open-source, freely replicable, and designed for dissemination through existing networks — including state education agencies and the Council of Chief State School Officers (CCSSO) collaborative network.

Why This Project — Why Now
A Gap No One Has Filled

Existing AI auditing tools were built for hiring, finance, and criminal justice. Education-specific tools remain either vendor-controlled or inaccessible to communities. This framework is the first designed from the ground up for educators and advocates in Southern schools.

A Researcher Built for This Work

Dr. Deas spent years inside the algorithmic systems now entering schools — developing accountability frameworks at Twitter and LinkedIn before those tools existed in classrooms. Combined with two decades in Southern education policy and authentic community roots, this is not outside-in research.

Intended Outcomes
1
Published open-source toolkit — a complete, freely available equity auditing framework with guides, templates, and protocols usable without programming skills.
2
Community documentation archive — qualitative accounts of AI impacts on students and families in Southern schools, forming an evidence base for advocacy and policy.
3
State agency adoption pathways — dissemination through active consulting relationships with Georgia DOE, Pennsylvania DOE, and Texas Education Agency, plus the CCSSO network.
4
Policy brief and practitioner guide — translating research findings into concrete recommendations for district procurement, vendor accountability, and state AI governance.
5
Replicable methodology — a Community-Based Participatory Action Research (CBPAR)-grounded research approach other communities can adapt, with SEF support for scale and sustainability beyond the initial fellowship year.
Funding & Fiscal Sponsorship

The Southern Education Foundation — the nation's oldest education justice organization — serves as fiscal sponsor for this project as part of its inaugural Research Fellows cohort. This means foundations can fund the work directly through SEF, satisfying organizational grant requirements while supporting independent research with maximum flexibility. Dr. Deas leads the work; SEF provides institutional infrastructure, risk management, and grantmaking support.
